##  Chronological Events
The Institute’s Role in the History of Space Exploration
Although the Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI) was not established until 1981, its roots can be traced back to Dr. Lyman Spitzer’s vision for what would become the Hubble Space Telescope in a document published in 1946. This interactive timeline invites viewers to explore the role the U.S. plays in space exploration, from the launch of the world’s first satellites to the discoveries led by Hubble and other space telescopes. Many items on the page expand: Click through to read more and see additional photos and videos. As STScI hits new milestones—like the launch and beginning of operations of the James Webb Space Telescope—events will be added to cover the breaking science results.

# Last Piece of NASA's Roman Space Telescope Completed and Delivered to NASA Goddard Space Flight Center
The Optical Telescope Assembly, which includes a 7.9-foot (2.4-meter) primary mirror, nine additional mirrors, and supporting structures and electronics, was delivered to the largest clean room at NASA Goddard Space Flight Center in Greenbelt, Maryland, where the observatory is being built.
Designed and constructed by L3Harris Technologies in Rochester, New York, the assembly incorporates key optics made available to NASA by the National Reconnaissance Office and carefully refined by L3Harris to meet Roman’s specifications.
The Optical Telescope Assembly will be installed onto Roman’s Instrument Carrier, a structure that will keep the telescope and its two instruments optically aligned.
Roman will fly with a Wide Field Instrument, a 300-megapixel infrared camera that will deliver expansive images, and a Coronagraph Instrument, a next-generation technology demonstration that will directly image planets and dusty disks surrounding other stars.

# Hubble Sees Possible Runaway Black Hole Creating a Trail of Stars
A team reviewing a Hubble Space Telescope image found a 200,000-light-year-long streak of light stretching far beyond a galaxy. Nothing like it had ever been seen before, and it couldn’t be easily explained. Later observations from the ground-based W. M. Keck Observatories confirmed the streak contains young blue stars along a chain that links the galaxy to a runway supermassive black hole. Rather than gobbling up stars ahead of it, the interpretation is that the black hole is plowing into gas in front of it, triggering new star formation along a narrow corridor.
How was it kicked out? Researchers point to the possibility of multiple black hole collisions. They suspect that two galaxies merged around 50 million years ago, bringing together two supermassive black holes that whirled around one another. Then another galaxy came along with its own supermassive black hole. The “dance” of these three black holes led to an unstable configuration. One of the black holes robbed momentum from the other two and was thrown out of the host galaxy.

# Webb Confirms Its First Exoplanet
Researchers using the James Webb Space Telescope identified and analyzed a rocky exoplanet that orbits a red dwarf star 41 light-years away. The team confirmed that LHS 475 b not only exists, but that it is a small, rocky planet that is almost exactly the same size as Earth.
The team chose to observe this target with Webb after carefully reviewing targets of interest from NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS), which hinted at its existence.
Among all operating telescopes, only Webb is capable of characterizing the atmospheres of Earth-sized exoplanets. Before Webb, researchers typically targeted planets the size of Jupiter, which is 11 times wider than Earth. Webb will help researchers discover more planets and gain far more information about Earth-like planets elsewhere in our Milky Way galaxy.

# Webb’s First Images Display the Universe in a New Light
On the evening of July 11, 2022, President Joe Biden unveiled the first full-color image from the James Webb Space Telescope. The near-infrared-light view of galaxy cluster SMACS 0723 is the deepest and sharpest image taken of the distant universe to date. The following morning, full-color images and spectroscopic data for an additional four targets were released, each revealing cosmic features that had never been captured in such detail.
Webb’s first observations demonstrated the telescope’s ability to finely detail the universe through cosmic history. These five images showed the wide array of capabilities of all Webb’s state-of-the-art cameras and spectrographs.
The release of Webb’s first images and spectra kicked off Webb’s science operations, with astronomers around the world beginning to study many different objects, from nearby planets to distant galaxies in the early universe.

# NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope Launches
NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope launched at 7:20 a.m. EST on an Ariane 5 rocket from Europe's Spaceport in French Guiana, South America. Ground teams began receiving telemetry data from Webb about five minutes after launch. The Arianespace Ariane 5 rocket performed as expected, separating from the observatory 27 minutes into the flight. The observatory was released at an altitude of approximately 75 miles (120 kilometers).
Approximately 30 minutes after launch, the Webb Telescope’s unique solar array unfolded, and mission managers confirmed that the array was providing power to the observatory. Within the telescope, there are four state-of-the-art instruments with highly sensitive infrared detectors that will study infrared light from celestial objects at unprecedented resolutions. Webb will deliver its first images at the end of a six-month commissioning period.
The James Webb Space Telescope, built in collaboration between NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency, will seek the light from the first galaxies in the early universe, study how galaxies and stars change over cosmic time, observe planets orbiting other stars, called exoplanets, and even study our own solar system.

# Decadal Survey Proposes New Missions and Programs
Once a decade, the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine seeks input from the U.S. astronomical community, requesting that scientists identify priorities in astronomy and astrophysics, and develop a comprehensive strategy for the upcoming decade. # Hubble Launches Large Ultraviolet-Light Survey of Nearby Stars
STScI launched an ambitious new initiative with the Hubble Space Telescope called ULLYSES (Ultraviolet Legacy Library of Young Stars as Essential Standards), a comprehensive program that will observe roughly 300 low-mass young stars and massive young stars in ultraviolet light. Due to its location above Earth’s atmosphere and its sensitive instruments, Hubble is the only observatory currently capable of making a detailed study of stars in ultraviolet light in high resolution.
As the data are gathered over three years, institute staff will create a spectral library of stellar “templates,” ensuring a legacy dataset for a wide range of astrophysical topics and ongoing research.
Stars span a broad range of sizes, ages, and temperatures—from diminutive red, cool, low-mass stars to opulent blue, hot, massive stars. (Our sun is roughly midway between these populations.) Since stars are the universe’s building blocks for immense galaxies, astronomers are always seeking a much better understanding of their formation, development, and impact on their surroundings making these data critical benchmarks.

# NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope Passes Comprehensive Systems Test
NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope successfully underwent a comprehensive systems test, the first full systems evaluation that has ever been run on the assembled observatory. Similar performance evaluations have been completed in Webb’s history, using simulations and surrogates to infer data about pieces of the spacecraft that had not yet been assembled. Now that Webb is fully built, simulations and simulators are no longer needed, and engineers confidently assessed both its software and electronic performance.

Webb is comprised of many components that must all communicate and work in unison to achieve mission success. Systems evaluations like these ensure that this happens by verifying and validating that each of them is communicating and performing as designed.

# Hubble Finds Best Evidence of an Elusive Mid-Sized Black Hole
Astronomers used Hubble to confirm evidence that an intense X-ray glow in a distant galaxy could be traced back to a mid-sized black hole. Weighing in at about 50,000 times the mass of our sun, the black hole is smaller than the supermassive black holes (at millions or billions of solar masses), but larger than stellar-mass black holes formed by the collapse of a massive star.
Intermediate-mass black holes are a long-sought “missing link” in black hole evolution. Though there have been a few other candidates, researchers consider these new observations the strongest evidence yet for mid-sized black holes in the universe.
It took the combined power of NASA’s Chandra X-ray Observatory, ESA’s XMM-Newton mission, and Hubble to nail down the cosmic beast. Hubble was pointed at the previously identified X-ray source to resolve its precise location. Deep, high-resolution imaging provided strong evidence that the X-rays emanated not from an isolated source in our galaxy, but instead in a distant, dense star cluster on the outskirts of another galaxy. The X-rays are generated as a shredded star’s superheated gas flows into the black hole, but before that material dips below the event horizon—the point beyond which any light emitted is forever hidden from our view.

# NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) Launches
NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) will survey the entire sky over the course of two years to make observations of 200,000 of the brightest stars near the Sun to find exoplanets using the transit method—measuring the detection of a planet when it passes in front of a star. Scientists expect the mission will catalog thousands of planet candidates and vastly increase the current number of known exoplanets.
The stars TESS will study are 30 to 100 times brighter than those NASA’s Kepler/K2 mission studied, which will enable easier follow-up observations with both ground- and space-based telescopes. TESS will also cover a sky area 400 times larger than that monitored by Kepler.
This rich set of targets will provide researchers opportunities for more comprehensive follow-up studies. All of TESS’s data is stored in the Space Telescope Science Institute’s Barbara A. Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (MAST).

# Gravitational and Light Waves Detected from the Same Celestial Event for the First Time
In an unprecedented collaboration, scientists worldwide combined the newly proven gravitational wave observatories LIGO and Virgo with telescope observations to view the source of a gravitational wave for the first time. Two seconds after LIGO detected a gravitational wave, the Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope detected a short, bright gamma ray burst.
The Swift, Chandra, and Hubble missions all followed up with observations in their respective light wavelengths. Across the globe, telescopes turned toward the source in hopes of seeing the quickly fading light signature. Together they confirmed the source as the collision of two neutron stars.
It was the first time telescopes and gravitational wave detectors had been used together to study one astronomical event. Astronomers anticipate that this is the first of many such detections, a new multi-method approach to observing the cosmos and decoding the messages it has for us.

# Discovery of the TRAPPIST-1 Solar System
NASA’s Spitzer Space Telescope revealed the first known system of seven Earth-sized planets around a single star. The system is named TRAPPIST-1, for the Transiting Planets and Planetesimals Small Telescope (TRAPPIST) in Chile, and is located about 40 light-years (235 trillion miles) from Earth.

Finding so many Earth-like exoplanets in the habitable zone was an exciting milestone toward one of NASA’s top science priorities: investigating the question, Are we alone?

# NASA’s Ambitious Frontier Fields Program Begins
In October 2013, scientists embarked on a three-year program to use galaxy clusters as natural lenses to see back to within 500 million years of the big bang. In collaboration with the Chandra X-ray Observatory and the infrared Spitzer Space Telescope, Hubble spent more than 630 hours peering at six galaxy clusters and six parallel fields—neighboring portions of the sky without galaxy clusters.
Galaxy clusters consist of hundreds to thousands of galaxies. The immense mass of stars, gas, and dark matter that make up the chosen clusters warps space-time, causing passing light to be redirected, magnified, and distorted. The effect, known as gravitational lensing, allows us to see galaxies more than 13 billion light-years away, far beyond the clusters.
The study of gravitationally lensed galaxies has been important not only for our understanding of the deep universe and distant past, but also of dark matter, the invisible substance that makes up more than 80 percent of the matter in the universe.

# eXtreme Deep Field
With the eXtreme Deep Field, or XDF, astronomers improved on Hubble’s already-remarkable achievements in imaging the beginnings of the universe. The XDF was assembled by combining 10 years of NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope photographs taken of a patch of sky at the center of the original Hubble Ultra Deep Field (2004).
The XDF image reaches much fainter galaxies and includes very deep exposures in red light from Hubble’s recently installed infrared camera, enabling new studies of the earliest galaxies. More than 2,000 images of the same field were taken with Hubble’s two premier cameras—the Advanced Camera for Surveys and the Wide Field Camera 3, which extends Hubble’s vision into near-infrared light—and combined to make the XDF.
The universe is 13.7 billion years old, and the XDF revealed galaxies that span back 13.2 billion years. To put this achievement in perspective, before Hubble was launched in 1990, astronomers could barely see galaxies to 7 billion light-years away.

# STScI Dark Energy Researcher Shares 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics
On October 4, 2011, the Nobel Prize for Physics was awarded to Saul Perlmutter of UC Berkeley, Brian Schmidt of Australian National University, and Adam Riess of the Space Telescope Science Institute for their work in the discovery of dark energy.
In the mid-1990s, the three astrophysicists were members of two research teams studying distant supernovae in an effort to determine how fast the expansion of the universe is decreasing over time. In 1998, each team reported its surprising—and independently determined—result: the expansion rate is actually increasing. The discovery that the universe is accelerating, propelled by the repulsive force of a mysterious, as-yet-unexplained dark energy, fundamentally changed our understanding of the universe: what it is made of, how it works, and how it has and will change over time.
Riess was the second staff member at STScI to be awarded a Nobel Prize. The first was awarded to STScI’s first director, Dr. Riccardo Giacconi, in 2002.

# NASA’s Kepler Mission Launches
NASA’s first planet-hunting spacecraft continuously monitored a patch of space that contains 150,000 stars like the Sun for indications of Earth-sized planets from 2009 to 2013. Kepler, which observed infrared light, explored the structure and diversity of planetary systems, determined the percentage of terrestrial and larger planets that are in or near the habitable zone of a variety of stars, and defined the distribution of sizes and shapes of the orbits of these planets.
When its second reaction wheel stopped functioning in 2013, the mission was transformed and renamed K2. The Kepler mission detected over 4,700 candidate exoplanets, with more than 2,300 confirmed. By 2018, K2 detected more than 500 candidates and confirmed over 350.

# Scientists Report First Direct Observation of an Exoplanet in Visible Light
In 2004, photographs revealed a wide disk of debris surrounding the bright, hot star Fomalhaut. Within this disk were a number of bright spots, which astronomers recognized as potential planets. On observing the disk with Hubble again in 2006, they discovered that one of those spots had moved—evidence that the object could indeed be a planet.
Although many exoplanet discoveries had been reported before 2008, all had been detected using indirect methods, such as transit (measuring the change in brightness of a star as a planet passes in front of it). None had been photographed directly. Fomalhaut b was the first to be imaged directly in visible light. Its discovery was announced alongside that of several other planets, directly imaged in infrared light using ground-based telescopes. The observations of Fomalhaut b demonstrated the power of Hubble’s cameras as well as its coronagraph, which was used to block the light from the star, making it possible to see the faint planet from a distance of 23 light-years.
The true nature of the object is still being debated in ongoing research, and follow-up observations may unravel this mystery.

# Hubble Makes First Detection of Organic Molecules on an Exoplanet
In April and May of 2007, Hubble was turned toward the star HD 189733 and its Jupiter-sized planet, HD 189733 b, 63 light-years away in the constellation Vulpecula. Using its Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrograph (NICMOS), Hubble analyzed the starlight traveling through the planet's atmosphere as it passed in front of the star once every 2.2 days. The results, announced in 2008, revealed something never before detected in an exoplanet atmosphere: a clear signature for the organic molecule methane, CH4.
Though the discovery did not provide evidence for the existence of extraterrestrial life—methane can be formed by abiotic processes and HD 189733 b is too hot to support life as we know it—it was an important step in the search for habitable planets outside our solar system. The discovery showed that telescopes like Hubble can be used successfully to analyze atmospheres of distant planets.

August 24, 2006
# Pluto Is Reclassified as a Kuiper Belt Object
Astronomers at the International Astronomical Union’s meeting in the Czech Republic voted to reclassify Pluto and Zena as dwarf planets. Before the vote, the meeting set a new definition for planets in our solar system, which had been debated for years.
A planet is an object in orbit around the Sun that is large enough (massive enough) to have its self-gravity pull itself into a round (or near-spherical) shape. In addition, a planet orbits in a clear path around the Sun. If any object ventures near the orbit of a planet, it will either collide with the planet, and thereby be accreted, or be ejected into another orbit.
Kuiper Belt Objects, like Pluto and Xena, were disqualified since they do not meet the third requirement—the Kuiper Belt is full of asteroids and comets, and these dwarf planets have not sufficiently cleared the neighborhoods in which they orbit.

# Hubble Captures Images of ‘Tenth Planet’ Slightly Larger Than Pluto
The Hubble Space Telescope, which observed the Kuiper Belt object Xena in December 2005, revealed to astronomers that it is approximately 5 percent larger than Pluto. Hubble observations yielded a diameter of 1,490 miles (with an uncertainty of 60 miles), while it measured Pluto's diameter as 1,422 miles. Previous ground-based observations suggested that Xena was about 30 percent greater in diameter. Xena is located about 10 billion miles away and amounts to 1.5 pixels across in Hubble's view, which is enough to precisely make a size measurement.
The team, led by Dr. Mike Brown, planned to use Hubble and other telescopes to study additional Kuiper Belt objects that are almost as large as Pluto and Xena. Finding that the largest known Kuiper Belt object is a virtual twin to Pluto further complicated the debate about whether to categorize the large icy worlds that dwell in the Kuiper Belt as planets. If Pluto were considered to be the minimum size for a planet, Xena would also fulfill the criterion.

# Astronomers Announce Discovery of Convincing Evidence for Dark Matter
Less than 20 percent of the matter in the universe is normal matter: protons, neutrons, and electrons that make up stars, gas, dust, and other materials. The remaining 80% is dark matter. Dark matter does not reflect or emit any type of light and can be detected based only on the gravitational influence it exerts on normal matter and on light.
In 2006, the Chandra X-Ray Observatory, working in collaboration with Hubble and other telescopes, revealed the most direct evidence yet that dark matter does exist. Observations of a galaxy cluster known as the Bullet Cluster showed that there is not enough mass in the normal matter to explain the motions of the galaxies in the cluster. Without dark matter holding them together, the galaxies and hot gas would have quickly flown apart. In addition, the distribution of mass in the cluster does not coincide with the distribution of normal matter. These observations helped rule out a competing idea that the effects attributed to dark matter could be the result of an incomplete understanding of gravity.

# Hubble Ultra Deep Field
On March 9, 2004, astronomers at STScI unveiled the deepest portrait of the visible universe ever achieved: the Hubble Ultra Deep Field (HUDF). A million-second-long exposure revealed the faint first galaxies that dispelled the “dark ages” after the big bang. Gone are the majestic spiral and elliptical galaxies we have come to recognize today, replaced with strange shapes of galaxies in a “toddler” stage of chaotic development, when order and structure were just beginning to emerge in the universe.
The ancient light seen in the HUDF cannot be viewed by ground-based telescopes, or even in Hubble’s previous faraway looks, called the Hubble Deep Fields. The HUDF field contains an estimated 10,000 galaxies and is actually two separate images taken by Hubble’s Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) and the Near Infrared Camera and Multi-object Spectrometer (NICMOS). The smallest, reddest galaxies may be among the most distant known, existing when the universe was just 800 million years old.

# The Spitzer Space Telescope Launches
After launching on a Delta II rocket from Florida, NASA’s Spitzer Space Telescope, named for astrophysicist Dr. Lyman Spitzer, settled into an Earth-trailing orbit. The observatory was purposefully designed to have two missions: one cool and one warm. The first used cryogenically cooled instruments to efficiently map large areas of the sky. After it depleted its liquid helium more than five year later, it switched to its second mission. Spitzer currently uses two IRAC channels to measure the near- to mid-infrared portion of the spectrum to study comets and asteroids in our solar system, planets orbiting other stars, and distant galaxies.
In 2017, the telescope discovered the first known system of seven Earth-size planets around a single star, TRAPPIST-1. Three of the planets are located in the habitable zone, the area around the parent star where a rocky planet is most likely to have liquid water. The finding set a new record for greatest number of habitable-zone planets found around a single star outside our solar system.
In January 2020, the spacecraft was decommissioned, ending more than 16 years of discovery. The Spitzer mission's complete data archive is publicly available through STScI's Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes and will continue to be mined for decades to come.

# NASA Unveils First Detailed Image of the Infant Universe
When seen with our eyes or viewed with telescopes that are designed to see only visible light, the space between distant stars and galaxies looks completely dark. But in fact, the entire sky is lit with a faint glow of very low-frequency light, a relic of the energy left over from the big bang that formed the universe some 13.8 billion years ago.
This glow, known as the cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB), was first detected in 1964. Between 1989 and 1993, the Cosmic Background Explorer satellite (COBE) mapped the radiation across the entire sky, revealing slight variations, or anisotropies, which reflect slight differences in temperature from place to place. In 2001, the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (WMAP) began mapping the CMB in even greater detail and in February 2003 NASA released the first WMAP map of the universe. The map was updated regularly until 2012, and details of the CMB were further refined with data from the Planck Space Telescope between 2009 and 2013.

# Hubble Looks Further with Help from Supercluster Abell 1689
On January 7, 2003, STScI released this Hubble image illustrating the effect of gravity warping space, and thus light, as predicted by Albert Einstein. Though Hubble had previously used the natural phenomena of massive bodies in space magnifying distant objects behind them—known as gravitational lensing—it had never been seen in such dramatic detail.
Astronomers pointed Hubble's Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) at Abell 1689, one of the most massive galaxy clusters known, for over 13 hours. The gravity of the supercluster's trillion stars—plus dark matter—act as a 2-million-light-year-wide “lens” in space, warping distant galaxies into clearly visible arcs amid the more distinct and bright foreground galaxies. The ACS is five times more sensitive than Hubble’s previous cameras; this image reveals 10 times more arcs than would be seen by a ground-based telescope.
The associated data from the observation provided material for the study of the age of the universe, its early eons, and the elusive nature of dark matter.

# STScI’s Founding Director Receives the Nobel Prize
Dr. Riccardo Giacconi, the “father of X-ray astronomy” and the first director of the Space Telescope Science Institute, received the Nobel Prize in Physics for his “pioneering contributions to astrophysics,” which led to the discovery of cosmic X-ray sources.
He discovered X-ray stars and the X-ray background in the 1960s, and conceived of and led the implementation of the Uhuru and High Energy Astronomy Observatory-2 (HEAO-2) X-ray observatories in the 1970s. In 1976, Giacconi and Dr. Harvey Tananbaum of the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics in Cambridge, Massachusetts, submitted a proposal to NASA to initiate the study and design of a large X-ray telescope. In 1977, work began on the program, which became the Chandra X-ray Observatory.
Giacconi, 71, received half the prize. Dr. Raymond Davis Jr., 87, of the University of Pennsylvania and Dr. Masatoshi Koshiba, 76, of the University of Tokyo shared the other half of the prize, worth about $1 million, for their research into cosmic neutrinos.

# NASA Names the Next Generation Space Telescope in Honor of James E. Webb
The Next Generation Space Telescope was renamed in honor of former NASA Administrator James E. Webb to recognize him as a champion for science. Although President John Kennedy had committed to landing a man on the moon before the end of the 1970s, Webb believed that NASA had to balance its efforts between human space flight and science.
James Webb was also a champion of a large space-based telescope mission, which would eventually become the Hubble Space Telescope. By the time he retired, NASA had launched more than 75 space-based science missions to study stars and galaxies, the Sun, and the environment of space above the Earth’s atmosphere.
Around the same time the Webb telescope was renamed, NASA selected the TRW corporation of Redondo Beach, California, (now Northrop Grumman Aerospace Systems) to build the Webb telescope. NASA also formed the Science Working Group to define and publish a rationale for the science requirements that will guide the Webb telescope’s formulation, design, and operation.

# Astronomers Announce First Direct Measurement of an Exoplanet Atmosphere
In May and June of 2000, astronomers focused Hubble’s Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph (STIS) on HD 209458 b, an exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star 150 light-years from Earth. Their goal: analyzing the colors of light given off by the star and measuring slight changes in the brightness of each color as the planet passed in front of the star. Atoms and molecules in the planet's atmosphere should absorb specific colors, leaving gaps in the light spectrum and providing evidence for the composition of the atmosphere.
In November 2001, astronomers reported success: The spectrum of starlight passing through the atmosphere of HD 209458 b showed clear evidence for sodium—the first direct measurement of an atmosphere outside our solar system.
The discovery showed that it is possible to detect extremely subtle differences in light, and analyze the atmospheres of planets in star systems hundreds of trillions of miles away.

# Remodeling Hubble in Space
In November 1999, Hubble went into safe mode after four of its six gyroscopes failed. As a result, NASA prioritized this servicing mission, sending astronauts on the space shuttle Discovery to replace all six gyroscopes, which accurately point the telescope at celestial targets.
Six days and three six-hour spacewalks later, the crew successfully replaced worn or outdated equipment, and performed several critical maintenance upgrades. They installed a computer that was 20 times faster than its predecessor and a digital data recorder that stored 10 times as much data. The crew also added an electronics enhancement kit, battery improvement kits, and new outer layers of thermal protection. Hubble was practically new.
The telescope’s first images, which were released in January, proved Hubble had a crystal-clear view. Astronomers pointed Hubble at two targets, NGC 2392 and Abell 2218, which gave scientists a clear view of a planetary nebula, as well as the first detailed look at the internal structure of a cluster of galaxies.

# Scientists Announce Completion of Hubble Key Project to Measure the Expansion Rate of the Universe
In the mid-1920s, Edwin Hubble recognized that the farther a galaxy is from Earth, the faster it appears to be moving away from us. This correlation between distance and apparent velocity shows that the universe is expanding, and is primary evidence for the big bang theory.
However, because of the difficulty of measuring precise distances to galaxies, the exact rate of expansion—the Hubble constant (H0)—remained uncertain. Without H0, it is impossible to determine how long the universe has been expanding, and thus how old it is.
With its powerful optics and position above the blurring effects of the atmosphere, the Hubble Space Telescope was uniquely suited for determining galactic distances. By correlating the distances of 18 galaxies to their apparent speeds, astronomers successfully narrowed the expansion rate down to an uncertainty of just 10 percent. The new constant tightened the possible age of the universe from 10 to 20 billion to 12 to 14 billion years old. Based on further refinements, we now think the universe is about 13.8 billion years old.

# Astronomers Announce that the Universe Is Accelerating, Driven by a Mysterious Dark Energy
Before 1997, astronomers assumed that the expansion rate of the universe must be decreasing. This made sense: The gravitational force exerted by the mass in the universe would be pulling on itself, slowing the expansion of space over time.
To measure this deceleration, two teams of astronomers embarked on studies to measure the brightness and redshift of distant supernovae. Brightness is directly related to a supernova’s distance, while redshift is a function of its apparent velocity. Combined with other studies, these data could be used to determine the change in the expansion rate of the universe over time.
But when they analyzed the data, the astronomers were astounded to discover that the universe is actually expanding at an _increasing_ rate. They reasoned that the source of this unexpected acceleration, which they called dark energy, must oppose gravity. The result fundamentally changed our understanding of the universe, and astronomers now estimate that roughly 70% of the matter and energy in the universe is dark energy.

# A Comprehensive Data Archive Is Established
The Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI) was already operating an efficient archive for distribution of the Hubble Space Telescope data when the International Ultraviolet Explorer (IUE) mission ended and NASA began to search for a permanent home for its data. # The Dressler Report: Recommending the Next Generation Space Telescope
In 1993, just three years into the Hubble mission, the Association of Universities for Research in Astronomy (AURA) established the HST & Beyond Committee to consider the scientific goals and technological needs of the astronomical community following the planned decommissioning of Hubble in the early 21st century.

# Hubble Reveals the Surface of Pluto
Astronomers obtained clear pictures of Pluto’s surface for the first time with the Hubble Space Telescope, revealing nearly the entire surface as it rotated over 6.4 days. Pluto is two-thirds the size of Earth’s Moon and 1,200 times farther away.
The images revealed distinctive features, including a ragged northern polar cap bisected by a dark strip, a bright spot seen rotating with the planet, a cluster of dark spots, and a bright linear marking. # Existence of Supermassive Black Holes Confirmed
STScI astronomer Holland Ford and Richard Harms of the Applied Research Corp. used the Hubble Space Telescope’s Wide Field Planetary Camera-2 (in planetary camera mode) to image a whirlpool of hot gas orbiting around the center of the giant elliptical galaxy M87. # NASA’s Compton Gamma-Ray Observatory Launches
Before NASA’s Compton Gamma Ray Observatory (CGRO) launched, it was not known if gamma rays only resulted from local, modest energy events. CGRO found that gamma-ray bursts are scattered evenly throughout the sky, proving that the bursts came from various cosmological distances.
The observatory made many other significant observations, including evidence of supermassive black holes, quasars, and supernova remnants. It also discovered blazars, a class of quasars that shine brightest in the gamma-ray range.
In 1999, the instrument cued a computer-controlled telescope at Las Alamos National Laboratory in New Mexico within 20 seconds of registering a burst. This experiment proved that its newly automated process worked, and CGRO sent regular gamma-ray burst alerts to inform the Hubble Space Telescope, the Chandra X-ray Observatory, and ground-based observatories around the world.
After one of CGRO’s gyroscopes failed in November 1999, NASA deorbited the satellite. It reentered Earth’s atmosphere June 4, 2000.

# Flaw in Hubble's Primary Mirror
After analyzing the Hubble Space Telescope’s first images, scientists realized the primary mirror had a flaw known as spherical aberration. An investigation revealed that during its manufacture, the outer edge of the mirror was ground too flat by a depth of 4 microns (roughly equal to one-50th the thickness of a human hair). The flaw scattered the light reflected by cosmic objects, resulting in fuzzy images.
STScI staff and their partners at NASA immediately began experimenting with algorithms to resolve and make use of Hubble’s data and imagery, pushing forward image processing technology. Less than two months later, STScI convened an expert workshop on the issue.
While Hubble was not returning the image quality expected, it was still providing results that were impossible for ground-based telescopes, with high resolution and spectra that led to new research. Year-one science presentations at STScI covered planets, hot stars, cool stars, chemically peculiar stars, stellar systems, gravitationally lensed quasars, and quasar absorption systems.

# Creating a New Path for Scientific Research
The Hubble Fellowship Program was conceived by the institute’s first director, Dr. Riccardo Giacconi, as part of the vision of how STScI could support the astronomical community. As prize fellows, the recipients are free to develop an independent research program at any institution within the U.S.

# Guide Star Catalog Published
Collecting data from stars and other astronomical targets requires knowledge of their precise location in the sky. Because the apparent position of a target changes as Hubble orbits, is important to have the ability to lock onto the target during observations. A team at STScI, led by Dr. Barry Lasker, developed the Guide Star Catalog (GSC) to aid in accurately pointing the telescope.
The team began by scanning and digitizing 1,477 sky survey plates taken with ground-based telescopes in California and Australia. Precise location and brightness information of nearly 19 million celestial objects in the resulting Digitized Sky Survey (DSS) was then compiled to form the GSC. At the same time, the Guide Star Selection System (GSSS) was developed to make it possible for Hubble to use the position and brightness information to lock on to specific stars and guide the telescope.
The GSC is regularly updated and continues to be used by ground- and space-based observatories. Visit [MAST](https://archive.stsci.edu/gsc/) to view the current catalog, which contains about 1 billion objects.

# Dedicating the Hubble Space Telescope
In 1923, Astronomer Edwin Powell Hubble spent months collecting data about the largest known spiral nebula at the Mt. Wilson Observatory in California, which led him to conclude that Andromeda is not a nebula, but a galaxy and, most importantly, that the Milky Way is only one of the galaxies in our universe. With these findings, he changed how we view our place in space.
He was able to make these calculations by measuring the variation of the luminosity (the length of time it takes a star to go from bright to dim and back again) of Cepheid variable stars in the Andromeda Galaxy based on a formula developed in 1912 by Harvard “computer” Henrietta Leavitt.
Hubble calculated that Andromeda is approximately 900,000 light-years away, more than eight times the distance to the farthest known stars in the Milky Way, which conclusively proved that Andromeda is a separate star system and the Milky Way is not the sum of the universe.
For this reason—and his many other accomplishments as an astronomer—NASA formally named the space telescope after him in 1983.

# The National Academy of Sciences Publishes ‘Institutional Arrangements for the Space Telescope’
To ensure the health and longevity of the ambitious Space Telescope project, NASA requested that the National Academy of Sciences organize a committee to study and report on the type of institution that should support it.
The study participants—led by Dr. Donald F. Hornig and made up of astronomers who operated ground-based observatories, conducted space-based experiments, and processed digital data—recommended founding an independent organization known as the Space Telescope Science Institute to provide not only long-term guidance and support for the telescope, but also to engage astronomers around the world.
Specifically, they recommended designing an institute operated by a broad-based consortium of universities and nonprofit institutions to encourage collaboration, and recommended employing sufficient staff to allocate time to use the telescope, carry out its science operations, generate new capabilities for the telescope, and reduce and broadly disseminate its data.
“No one can predict what new kinds of objects, what new concepts, what new enigmas [the Space Telescope] will yield, nor what particular combination of ground-based observations and [Space Telescope] observations may yield them,” the study participants reasoned. “Astronomers not yet born may provide its crowning discoveries.”

# Formalizing the Idea for a Space-based Telescope
Astronomers first put forth the idea to construct and launch a Large Space Telescope (LST) during a Space Science Board meeting in Woods Hole, Massachusetts, in the summer of 1965. Around the same time, NASA conducted studies to determine the feasibility of a project like this. It wasn’t until a series of scientific reports came out in the following years that the concept was translated into a substantial report published by the National Academy of Sciences, titled “Scientific Uses of the Large Space Telescope.”
This report arrived at several significant conclusions. First, the LST would make a major contribution to our knowledge of cosmology, particularly our understanding of the structure, scale, and evolution of the universe. And, notably, that the telescope should work in concert with other, smaller space-based telescopes, as well as a number of ground-based instruments.

# NASA Names Nancy Grace Roman Its First Chief of Astronomy
Dr. Nancy Grace Roman joined NASA as its first chief of astronomy and relativity programs in the Office of Space Science when the agency was in its first year of operations. She quickly became a champion for the Large Space Telescope, now known as the Hubble Space Telescope, which astronomers proposed be the [first space-based observatory](https://www.stsci.edu/who-we-are/our-history/stsci-timeline.html#h4-96dff5a9-9b9a-4d37-b0af-b1be69b45be3) capable of observing in ultraviolet and visible light.
In 1960, Roman brought together astronomers from all over the U.S. to meet with NASA engineers to revise the telescope’s designs, ensure that it met engineering requirements, and confirm it would be capable of capturing and returning data from a low-Earth orbit.
The team went on to develop charge coupled devices (CCDs) that included a chloramine coating to guarantee the telescope would not only be sensitive to visible light, but also to ultraviolet light, and could be operated remotely.
During her time at NASA, she oversaw the development of three orbiting solar observatories and several astronomical observatories, including the Copernicus satellite, which observed ultraviolet and X-ray light, and the International Ultraviolet Explorer (IUE), which observed in ultraviolet light.
Dr. Roman retired in 1979, but continued to contribute to astronomy through other roles at NASA, universities, and as a volunteer, sharing her knowledge of astronomy with the public until her death in 2018.
NASA’s Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, formerly known as the Wide Field Infrared Survey Telescope (WIFRST), is named in honor of Dr. Roman.

# ‘Astronomical Advantages of an Extra-terrestrial Observatory’
Although astronomers have used telescopes since the 1600s, the Earth’s atmosphere limits the data we can observe by distorting light. In 1946, Lyman Spitzer proposed designing, building, and launching an “extra-terrestrial observatory” into Earth’s orbit—but his paper was printed as an appendix of a long, detailed document prepared for the Project RAND (an organization formed after World War II to connect military planning with research and development decisions) of the Douglas Aircraft Company.
Although his paper did not appear in a peer-reviewed astronomical journal and was not widely distributed, writing the proposal had an influential effect on the author, convincing him that a large space telescope would revolutionize astronomy—and that this observatory could be launched in his lifetime. When he became the director of the Princeton University Observatory in 1947, he began a program to work toward this significant, long-term goal. The paper was reprinted with a postscript in _Astronomy Quarterly_ in 1990 after the Hubble Space Telescope launched.

# Edwin Hubble Definitively Demonstrates the Universe Is Expanding
Edwin Hubble transformed our view of the cosmos by publishing a paper in the _Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences_ that provided evidence that the universe is constantly expanding. By collecting data at the Mt. Wilson Observatory in California, Hubble determined distances to galaxies outside the Milky Way, which led him to find a linear relationship between an object’s velocity and its distance from us. His work followed that of French astronomer Georges Lemaître, who published observational and theoretical evidence reaching the same conclusion in 1927. The findings, now known as the Hubble–Lemaître Law, indicate that galaxies are moving away from one another at speeds proportional to their distance.
